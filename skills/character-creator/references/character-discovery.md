# Character Discovery

Sources: Bancroft (Creating Characters with Personality), Tillman (Creative Character Design), Sloan (Virtual Character Design for Games), Eggleston (Character Design Made Easy), Harder (Creative Character Design for Games and Animation)

---

This file covers the discovery and personality profiling phase of character creation — the structured interview that transforms a vague character idea into a defined design brief. The output of this phase is a Character Essence Statement and a Personality Profile that feed directly into archetype selection and visual design decisions. Shape language, color theory, and AI generation are handled in separate reference files.

---

## The Discovery Interview

Conduct the interview in sequence. Record the user's answers verbatim before interpreting them. The user's own language — the words they choose, the references they reach for — is the raw material for every design decision that follows. Resist the urge to suggest answers or complete their sentences.

Ten questions is the full interview. For quick requests, Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4, and Q9 are the minimum viable set. Never skip Q9 (reference characters) or Q10 (anti-brief) — they reveal more than any direct question.

### Interview Modes

| Mode | Questions | Use When |
|------|-----------|----------|
| Full discovery | Q1–Q10 | New character, no prior brief, complex project |
| Standard | Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4, Q5, Q9, Q10 | Most requests; covers personality and constraints |
| Quick | Q1, Q2, Q3, Q9 | User has a clear concept and just needs visual direction |
| Revision | Q9, Q10 only | User has an existing character and wants to refine or redirect |

In Quick mode, infer Q4 (motivation) and Q5 (flaw) from the traits given in Q3 and the reference characters in Q9. Flag any inferences explicitly so the user can correct them before visual work begins.

---

### Q1 — Role in Story or Game

"What is this character's role? Are they a protagonist, antagonist, mentor, sidekick, comic relief, or something else?"

**Why it matters:** Role determines the character's relationship to the audience and to other characters. A protagonist must be legible and relatable at first glance — the audience needs to attach to them quickly. An antagonist can afford ambiguity or menace. A mentor signals wisdom through visual weight and restraint. Bancroft identifies role as the first filter on every design decision: the same personality expressed in a protagonist reads differently than in a villain. Establish role before anything else.

---

### Q2 — The Essence Sentence

"Describe this character in one sentence — who they are at their core."

**Why it matters:** Forces the user to distill before elaborating. Vague answers ("she's kind of mysterious and also really powerful") signal that the character concept is not yet resolved — a problem to surface now, not after visual development begins. A strong essence sentence contains a tension: "a disgraced soldier who still believes in honor" or "a child who sees the world as it should be, not as it is." That tension is the character. If the user cannot produce one sentence, ask: "What is the one thing that is always true about this character, no matter what situation they're in?"

---

### Q3 — Personality Traits

"List 3-5 personality traits. Include at least one flaw or contradiction — not just positive qualities."

**Why it matters:** Eggleston's attribute system distinguishes between surface traits (what others observe) and deep traits (what drives behavior under pressure). A list of only positive traits produces a flat character with no visual tension. The flaw is where the design lives: a character who is brave but reckless reads differently than one who is brave and methodical. Push for specificity — "stubborn" is more useful than "strong-willed," "sardonic" more useful than "funny." The flaw also anchors the character's arc and informs expression sheet priorities.

---

### Q4 — Core Motivation

"What does this character want most? What are they actively pursuing or protecting?"

**Why it matters:** Motivation is the engine of the character. Sloan's research on player-character attachment shows that characters with legible motivations generate stronger emotional investment than those defined purely by appearance. Motivation also determines posture, expression defaults, and costume function — a character motivated by protection carries themselves differently than one motivated by recognition. Distinguish between the external want (what they're chasing) and the internal need (what they actually require to grow). Both inform design.

---

### Q5 — Greatest Fear or Internal Flaw

"What is this character's greatest fear, or the internal flaw that holds them back?"

**Why it matters:** The flaw creates visual and narrative contrast with the motivation. A character who wants power but fears being seen as weak has a built-in tension that shapes every expression, every costume choice, every silhouette decision. Harder notes that characters without visible vulnerability read as icons rather than individuals — compelling for logos, limiting for storytelling. The fear also determines the character's stress expression, which is one of the six mandatory expressions on a professional character sheet.

---

### Q6 — Physical Appearance

"Describe their physical appearance in broad strokes: approximate age, build, and any distinguishing features that feel essential to who they are."

**Why it matters:** Broad strokes only at this stage — specific proportions and construction come in the visual design phase. What matters here is which physical qualities the user considers essential versus incidental. If they immediately mention a scar, that scar carries narrative weight. If they describe build before face, physicality is central to the character's identity. Age range determines proportion system: realistic (7-8 heads), heroic (8-9 heads), stylized (5-6 heads), or chibi (2-3 heads). Note what the user does not mention — absence is as informative as presence.

---

### Q7 — World and Setting

"What world or setting does this character exist in? Fantasy, science fiction, contemporary, historical, or something else?"

**Why it matters:** Setting constrains and enables the entire visual vocabulary. A character in a grimdark fantasy world operates under different design rules than the same personality type in a bright Saturday-morning cartoon. Setting determines costume logic, technology level, color palette range, and the visual conventions the audience will bring to the work. Tillman emphasizes that archetype expression is always filtered through genre — a Trickster in a cyberpunk setting looks nothing like a Trickster in a fairy tale, even if the underlying personality profile is identical.

---

### Q8 — Art Style

"What art style should this character be rendered in? Anime, realistic, semi-realistic, pixel art, cartoon, painterly, or something else?"

**Why it matters:** Art style is not decoration — it is a design constraint that determines line weight, color saturation range, level of detail, and proportion conventions. Anime styles carry specific proportion and expression conventions that differ from Western animation. Pixel art imposes a resolution constraint that forces simplification. Realistic styles demand anatomical plausibility that stylized work can ignore. Establish style before visual development begins, not after. If the user is uncertain, Q9 (reference characters) will often resolve this implicitly.

---

### Q9 — Reference Characters

"Name 2-3 existing characters — from any medium — that feel similar to what you want. What specifically do you like about each?"

**Why it matters:** Reference characters reveal unstated preferences faster than any direct question. A user who names Geralt of Rivia, Aragorn, and Joel from The Last of Us is communicating a specific emotional register — weathered, morally complex, physically imposing — that no amount of trait-listing would capture as precisely. The "what specifically" follow-up is critical: the user may admire Geralt's design but not his stoicism, or Aragorn's arc but not his visual style. Note which qualities they are borrowing and which they are not. Reference characters also calibrate the personality profile scoring in the next phase.

---

### Q10 — The Anti-Brief

"What must this character absolutely not look or feel like? Name a character, a style, or a quality you want to avoid."

**Why it matters:** The anti-brief defines the character's edges as precisely as the brief itself. A user who says "nothing like a generic anime protagonist with spiky hair" has eliminated an entire visual vocabulary. A user who says "not cute, not approachable, not safe" has defined the emotional register by exclusion. Bancroft identifies the anti-brief as the most reliable way to surface unstated requirements — clients and collaborators often know what they do not want more clearly than what they do want. Record the anti-brief verbatim and treat it as a hard constraint throughout visual development.

## Personality Profiling System

After the interview, score the character on five dimensions adapted from Aaker's Brand Personality framework. Do not ask the user to score directly — infer from their answers. The goal is a structured profile that can be handed off to archetype selection and visual design without further interpretation.

### The Five Dimensions

| Dimension | Low (1) | Neutral (3) | High (5) | Primary Inputs |
|-----------|---------|-------------|----------|----------------|
| **Sincerity** | Distant, guarded, ironic | Balanced | Warm, genuine, open | Q3 traits, Q4 motivation |
| **Excitement** | Steady, measured, restrained | Balanced | Bold, energetic, unpredictable | Q3 traits, Q5 flaw |
| **Competence** | Casual, improvised, uncertain | Balanced | Expert, disciplined, authoritative | Q1 role, Q4 motivation |
| **Sophistication** | Raw, accessible, unpolished | Balanced | Refined, controlled, elevated | Q6 appearance, Q7 setting |
| **Ruggedness** | Polished, urban, delicate | Balanced | Tough, weathered, physical | Q6 appearance, Q7 setting |

### Scoring from Interview Answers

**Sincerity** is high when: the user describes warmth, loyalty, or emotional openness in Q3; the motivation in Q4 is relational (protecting someone, belonging, being understood). Sincerity is low when: the character is described as guarded, cynical, or emotionally closed; the motivation is self-directed.

**Excitement** is high when: Q3 traits include impulsiveness, boldness, or unpredictability; the flaw in Q5 is excess (recklessness, obsession, volatility). Excitement is low when: the character is described as methodical, patient, or controlled; the role in Q1 is mentor or strategist.

**Competence** is high when: Q1 role is expert, leader, or authority figure; Q4 motivation involves mastery, achievement, or responsibility. Competence is low when: the character is defined by their inexperience, naivety, or reliance on others.

**Sophistication** is high when: Q6 describes refined appearance, controlled presentation, or elevated social position; Q7 setting is aristocratic, futuristic, or high-status. Sophistication is low when: the character is described as rough, common, or deliberately anti-establishment.

**Ruggedness** is high when: Q6 describes physical build, scars, weathering, or practical clothing; Q7 setting is wilderness, war, or survival. Ruggedness is low when: the character is described as elegant, precise, or physically delicate.

### Example Profiles by Character Type

| Character Type | Sincerity | Excitement | Competence | Sophistication | Ruggedness |
|----------------|-----------|------------|------------|----------------|------------|
| Grizzled warrior | 3 | 2 | 4 | 1 | 5 |
| Cunning trickster | 2 | 4 | 4 | 3 | 2 |
| Gentle healer | 5 | 1 | 3 | 2 | 1 |
| Cold-blooded villain | 1 | 2 | 5 | 4 | 2 |
| Naive hero | 5 | 4 | 2 | 1 | 2 |
| Wise mentor | 4 | 1 | 5 | 3 | 3 |
| Chaotic rebel | 2 | 5 | 2 | 1 | 4 |
| Noble ruler | 3 | 2 | 5 | 5 | 2 |

### Reading the Profile

The two highest-scoring dimensions define the character's primary personality territory. The lowest-scoring dimension defines what the character is explicitly not — this is as important as what they are.

A character scoring 5 on Competence and 1 on Sincerity (cold expert, calculating authority) reads differently than one scoring 5 on Competence and 5 on Sincerity (warm expert, trusted guide). The combination, not the individual scores, produces the character's emotional signature.

When two dimensions score equally high, the character has a dual nature — this is a feature, not a problem. A character with equal Excitement and Competence (the brilliant improviser) is more interesting than one who scores high on only one. Note the tension between the two dominant dimensions: it is often the source of the character's internal conflict.

When all five dimensions score near 3, the character is an Everyman — defined by ordinariness rather than extremity. This is a valid and powerful archetype, but it requires the flaw and motivation to carry more weight, since the personality profile alone will not differentiate the design.

## Archetype Quick-Select

The personality profile maps to one of twelve Jungian archetypes. Use the decision table to identify the primary archetype, then check for a compatible secondary archetype that adds depth without contradiction.

### Archetype Decision Table

| Dominant Dimensions | Primary Archetype | Compatible Secondary | Avoid |
|--------------------|-------------------|---------------------|-------|
| High Sincerity + Low Excitement | Innocent or Caregiver | Everyman | Outlaw, Ruler |
| High Excitement + High Ruggedness | Explorer or Outlaw | Hero | Innocent, Lover |
| High Competence + Low Ruggedness | Sage or Ruler | Creator | Jester, Innocent |
| High Sophistication + Low Sincerity | Lover or Ruler | Magician | Jester, Everyman |
| High Excitement + Low Sophistication | Jester or Outlaw | Explorer | Ruler, Sage |
| High Sincerity + High Competence | Caregiver or Sage | Everyman | Outlaw, Jester |
| High Excitement + High Competence | Hero or Magician | Creator | Innocent, Caregiver |
| High Ruggedness + High Sincerity | Explorer or Hero | Everyman | Lover, Ruler |
| High Sophistication + High Competence | Ruler or Magician | Sage | Jester, Innocent |
| Balanced across all dimensions | Everyman | Caregiver | Any extreme archetype |

### Archetype Reference Table

| Archetype | Core Drive | Typical Role | Example Characters |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Innocent | Purity, safety, goodness | Child hero, idealist, naive protagonist | Dorothy (Wizard of Oz), Forrest Gump, Totoro |
| Explorer | Freedom, discovery, authenticity | Wanderer, scout, outsider | Aragorn, Moana, Geralt of Rivia |
| Sage | Truth, knowledge, wisdom | Mentor, advisor, scholar | Gandalf, Yoda, Sherlock Holmes |
| Hero | Mastery, courage, achievement | Protagonist, champion, warrior | Achilles, Mulan, Master Chief |
| Outlaw | Liberation, disruption, revenge | Anti-hero, rebel, vigilante | Deadpool, V (V for Vendetta), Loki |
| Magician | Transformation, vision, power | Sorcerer, catalyst, visionary | Merlin, Doctor Strange, Elsa |
| Lover | Intimacy, passion, beauty | Romantic lead, devoted ally, seducer | Romeo, Daenerys, Poison Ivy |
| Jester | Joy, play, irreverence | Comic relief, trickster, chaos agent | Puck, Harley Quinn, Timon & Pumbaa |
| Everyman | Belonging, fairness, dignity | Relatable protagonist, underdog | Bilbo Baggins, Peter Parker, Samwise |
| Caregiver | Protection, nurturing, sacrifice | Healer, guardian, parent figure | Molly Weasley, Baymax, Aerith |
| Creator | Originality, craft, expression | Inventor, artist, builder | Tony Stark, Hephaestus, Violet Parr |
| Ruler | Order, authority, responsibility | Leader, king, commander | Mufasa, Cersei Lannister, Commander Shepard |

### Compatible and Incompatible Pairings

| Primary | Compatible Secondary | Rationale |
|---------|---------------------|-----------|
| Hero | Ruler | Adds authority and command to raw courage |
| Explorer | Outlaw | Adds defiance and edge to wandering freedom |
| Sage | Creator | Adds inventiveness to accumulated wisdom |
| Caregiver | Innocent | Adds purity and selflessness to nurturing |
| Magician | Creator | Adds craft and originality to transformation |
| Jester | Outlaw | Adds subversion and danger to playfulness |
| Outlaw | Explorer | Adds restlessness and purpose to rebellion |

Incompatible pairings to avoid: Innocent + Outlaw (contradictory moral registers), Ruler + Jester (authority undermines itself), Sage + Jester (wisdom and chaos cancel each other), Caregiver + Ruler (nurturing and domination conflict at the core).

## From Profile to Archetype: The Translation Step

Before selecting an archetype, verify that the profile is internally consistent. A profile with contradictory scores — high Sincerity and high Sophistication, for example — signals either a genuinely complex character or an unresolved concept. Distinguish between the two before proceeding.

**Genuinely complex:** The contradiction is intentional and the user can articulate it. A character who is warm and emotionally open (high Sincerity) but also controlled and elevated in presentation (high Sophistication) is a specific type — the aristocrat who genuinely cares, the mentor who maintains dignity without coldness. This is a valid profile. Note the tension and carry it into the archetype selection.

**Unresolved concept:** The user has not yet decided who the character is. High scores across incompatible dimensions without a clear rationale signal that the interview needs another pass. Return to Q2 (essence sentence) and Q5 (flaw) — these two answers usually resolve the contradiction.

Once the profile is consistent, the archetype selection is mechanical: run the decision table, identify the primary archetype, check for a compatible secondary. The archetype is not a cage — it is a starting point. A Hero archetype with a secondary Outlaw dimension is a different design problem than a Hero with a secondary Caregiver dimension, even if the personality scores are identical.

## Character Essence Statement

The Character Essence Statement is the single-sentence output of the discovery phase. It captures the character's archetype, role, motivation, and flaw in a form that can be handed off to any collaborator — writer, artist, animator — and produce consistent results.

### The Formula

```
A [archetype] [role] who [core motivation] but [fatal flaw or internal contradiction].
```

The "but" is not optional. A statement without a flaw describes an icon, not a character.

### Examples Across Genres

| Genre | Essence Statement |
|-------|------------------|
| Epic fantasy | A Hero warrior who fights to protect the innocent but cannot forgive himself for those he has failed to save. |
| Sci-fi thriller | A Sage scientist who pursues truth at any cost but has convinced herself that the ends justify the means. |
| Contemporary drama | An Everyman father who wants nothing more than his family's approval but sabotages every relationship that gets too close. |
| Dark fantasy | An Outlaw assassin who kills only those who deserve it but has lost the ability to feel anything when she does. |
| Children's adventure | An Innocent child who believes the world is fundamentally good but is slowly learning that belief requires courage to maintain. |
| Political drama | A Ruler commander who has built an empire on order and discipline but rules from fear because he has never learned to trust. |

The essence statement is not a logline or a pitch. It is a design constraint. Every visual decision — silhouette, color, expression defaults — should be traceable back to it.

## Discovery Red Flags

| Answer Pattern | What It Signals | How to Respond |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Only positive traits listed | Underdeveloped character concept | Ask: "What is the one thing about this character that gets them into trouble?" |
| Motivation is vague ("they want to be good") | No dramatic engine | Push for specificity: "Good at what? Good compared to whom? What would they sacrifice to achieve it?" |
| Reference characters contradict each other | Conflicting design intentions | Surface the contradiction: "These characters have very different emotional registers — which quality matters most to you?" |
| No answer to Q10 (anti-brief) | User has not thought about constraints | Ask: "Is there a character you've seen that felt wrong for reasons you couldn't quite articulate?" |
| Setting and personality are mismatched | Concept not yet integrated | Note the mismatch; it may be intentional (a sophisticated character in a rugged world) or a sign the concept needs more development |
| Essence sentence is a list of traits | User is describing, not defining | Reframe: "If this character had to make one impossible choice, what would it be?" |
| Reference characters are all from the same franchise | Narrow visual vocabulary | Expand: "What character from a completely different genre has a similar emotional quality?" |
| Flaw is external ("they're clumsy") | Surface-level characterization | Push deeper: "What internal belief or fear causes that behavior?" |

## Quality Gate

Before proceeding to visual design, verify these conditions are met. A discovery phase that fails any gate will produce visual decisions that cannot be defended or extended.

| Gate | Condition | If Failing |
|------|-----------|------------|
| Role clarity | Character's narrative function is unambiguous | Return to Q1; establish role before proceeding |
| Essence legibility | Essence statement contains a motivation and a flaw | Return to Q4 and Q5; resolve the missing element |
| Trait specificity | At least one flaw is named with behavioral specificity | Return to Q3; push past surface-level descriptors |
| Personality coherence | Dimension scores do not contradict each other | Return to Q3 and Q5; resolve the contradiction |
| Archetype fit | Selected archetype matches personality profile and role | Re-run the archetype decision table with corrected scores |
| Anti-brief defined | At least one hard visual or tonal constraint is recorded | Return to Q10; surface the constraint before visual work begins |
| Reference characters noted | At least two reference characters with specific qualities identified | Return to Q9; push for specificity on what is being borrowed |

A discovery phase that passes all seven gates is ready to hand off to archetype-visual-system.md for shape language and visual direction decisions.
