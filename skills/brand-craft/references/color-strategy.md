## 1. Color Psychology Quick Reference

Color is processed before shape, before words, before conscious thought. Up to 90% of snap judgments about products are based on color alone; color increases brand recognition by up to 80%. These associations operate through three mechanisms: biological (hardwired responses), cultural (learned by geography), and personal (individual memory). The table below covers the biological and Western-cultural layer — consult Section 3 for regional overrides.

| Color | Core Associations | Avoid When | Industries | Example Brands |
|-------|------------------|------------|------------|----------------|
| **Red** | Urgency, energy, passion, appetite | Brand needs to convey calm, trust, or premium restraint | Food, retail, entertainment, sports | Coca-Cola, Netflix, YouTube, Target |
| **Blue** | Trust, stability, intelligence, calm | Brand needs warmth, creativity, or disruption | Finance, technology, healthcare, B2B | IBM, PayPal, LinkedIn, Samsung |
| **Green** | Growth, health, nature, prosperity | Brand is in finance and wants to avoid "money" cliché | Health/wellness, food, environment, finance | Whole Foods, Starbucks, Spotify |
| **Yellow** | Optimism, warmth, attention, creativity | Brand targets premium or serious audiences | Food, children's products, creative, retail | McDonald's, IKEA, Snapchat, DHL |
| **Orange** | Enthusiasm, warmth, affordability, friendliness | Brand needs to signal luxury or authority | Food, retail, technology, creative | Amazon, Fanta, Etsy, Penguin Books |
| **Purple** | Luxury, wisdom, mystery, spirituality | Brand targets mass-market or rugged audiences | Beauty, luxury, spirituality, creative | Cadbury, Hallmark, Twitch, Crown Royal |
| **Black** | Sophistication, power, elegance, authority | Brand needs approachability or warmth | Luxury, fashion, premium technology | Chanel, Apple, Nike, Prada |
| **White** | Purity, simplicity, cleanliness, space | Brand needs warmth or richness | Healthcare, technology, luxury (as space) | Apple, Dove, Tesla, Muji |
| **Pink** | Romance, femininity, playfulness, nurturing | Brand targets audiences who reject gendered cues | Beauty, fashion, food, children | Barbie, Victoria's Secret, Glossier |
| **Brown** | Earth, reliability, warmth, authenticity | Brand needs modernity or energy | Food, outdoor, craft, heritage | UPS, Hershey's, Timberland |
| **Gold** | Prestige, success, achievement, warmth | Brand targets value-conscious or casual audiences | Luxury, finance, awards, premium tiers | Rolex, Versace, Johnnie Walker |

## 3. Cultural Color Considerations

Never finalize a palette for a global or regional brand without checking the primary market's cultural associations. The table below covers the most consequential differences. When a brand operates across multiple regions, default to the most universally safe interpretation or use color in ways that avoid the problematic association (e.g., use white as a background element rather than a primary brand color in East Asian markets).

| Color | Western (US/EU) | East Asia (China/Japan/Korea) | Middle East | Latin America | South Asia (India) |
|-------|----------------|-------------------------------|-------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **White** | Purity, weddings, cleanliness | Mourning, death, funerals | Purity, peace | Purity | Mourning (widows) |
| **Red** | Danger, passion, stop | Luck, prosperity, celebration | Danger, caution | Passion, energy | Purity, celebration |
| **Green** | Nature, go, money | Nature, growth | Sacred (Islam), paradise | Nature | Auspicious, fertility |
| **Yellow** | Caution, happiness | Imperial, sacred, royalty | Happiness, prosperity | Caution, warmth | Sacred, auspicious |
| **Purple** | Royalty, luxury | Mourning (some regions) | Wealth | Death, mourning (Brazil) | Sorrow |
| **Blue** | Trust, authority | Trust, immortality | Protection, heaven | Trust, calm | Universal trust |
| **Orange** | Affordable, energetic | Happiness, good luck | Strength, endurance | Warmth, energy | Sacred (saffron) |
| **Black** | Mourning, luxury, power | Neutral, water element | Mourning, evil | Mourning | Negative, evil |

**Practical rule:** Blue is the most globally safe primary color — it carries positive or neutral associations across virtually all major markets. Red is the highest-risk color for global brands due to its contradictory associations.

## 4. Color Harmony Rules

All harmony types derive from a single base hue on the color wheel. In HSL or OKLCH, harmony is a matter of rotating the hue angle. The choice of harmony type determines the emotional register of the palette before any specific color is chosen.

| Harmony Type | Construction | Emotional Effect | When to Use |
|-------------|-------------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Complementary** | Base hue + 180° | Maximum contrast, high energy, tension | CTAs, sports brands, retail urgency, attention-grabbing campaigns |
| **Analogous** | Base hue ± 30° (3 adjacent colors) | Harmonious, calm, natural, cohesive | Wellness, nature, approachable consumer brands, editorial |
| **Triadic** | Base hue + 120° + 240° | Vibrant, balanced, playful, diverse | Children's brands, creative industries, entertainment |
| **Split-Complementary** | Base hue + 150° and + 210° | High contrast with less tension than complementary | Versatile; good default for most brands needing contrast |
| **Tetradic (Square)** | Base hue + 90° + 180° + 270° | Rich, complex, full-spectrum | Advanced use only; requires one dominant color to avoid chaos |
| **Monochromatic** | Single hue, vary lightness and saturation | Sophisticated, cohesive, elegant, restrained | Luxury, minimalist brands, premium technology |

**Practical guidance:**
- Complementary palettes work best when one color dominates (60%) and the complement is used only as accent (10%).
- Analogous palettes feel natural but can lack contrast — add a neutral or a small complementary accent for CTAs.
- Triadic palettes require one dominant color; using all three equally creates visual noise.
- Monochromatic palettes depend entirely on a well-constructed shade scale — see Section 6.

## 5. The 60-30-10 Rule

The 60-30-10 rule is the foundational distribution principle for brand color application, borrowed from interior design. It prevents visual chaos by establishing a clear hierarchy before any specific colors are chosen.

```
60% — DOMINANT (Primary)
  Purpose: Backgrounds, large surfaces, overall mood
  Character: Most neutral or versatile color in the palette
  Examples: Page backgrounds, large containers, hero sections

30% — SECONDARY
  Purpose: Supporting elements, headers, cards, navigation
  Character: Creates visual interest and depth; complements primary
  Examples: Navigation bars, card backgrounds, section dividers

10% — ACCENT
  Purpose: CTAs, highlights, interactive elements, key data points
  Character: Bold, contrasting; creates focal points and drives action
  Examples: Buttons, links, active states, badges, price callouts
```

The 10% accent achieves maximum impact precisely because it is used sparingly. If the accent color appears on 40% of the page, it loses its ability to direct attention.

**Digital application mapping:**

| Surface | Percentage | Color Role |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| Page background | ~40% | Primary (lightest shade) |
| Cards, panels | ~20% | Primary (slightly darker) or neutral |
| Navigation, headers | ~30% | Secondary |
| Buttons, links, icons | ~10% | Accent |

## 6. Palette Construction from a Primary Color

Given a single hex value, derive a complete brand palette in five steps. This process works in HSL for conceptual clarity; use OKLCH (via culori) for perceptually uniform output in production.

### Step 1: Establish the Primary in HSL

Convert the chosen hex to HSL to expose the three parameters you will manipulate:

```
Example primary: #0E96E8
HSL equivalent:  HSL(204, 87%, 48%)
                 ↑     ↑     ↑
                 Hue   Sat   Lightness
```

### Step 2: Generate a 9-Shade Scale

Hold hue and saturation constant; vary lightness from near-white to near-black. Label shades 50–900 following Tailwind/Radix conventions.

| Token | Lightness | Hex (example) | Typical Use |
|-------|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| 50 | 97% | `#EFF8FF` | Page backgrounds |
| 100 | 93% | `#DBEFFE` | Card backgrounds |
| 200 | 85% | `#BAE0FD` | Borders, dividers |
| 300 | 73% | `#7CC8FB` | Disabled states |
| 400 | 62% | `#38AEF8` | Light interactive |
| **500** | **48%** | **`#0E96E8`** | **Primary brand color** |
| 600 | 40% | `#0278C7` | Hover states |
| 700 | 30% | `#035FA0` | Active states |
| 800 | 20% | `#074F82` | Dark backgrounds |
| 900 | 12% | `#0C3D61` | Near-black |

Reduce saturation slightly (by 5–10%) at the extreme ends (50 and 900) to prevent the lightest shade from reading as tinted and the darkest from reading as muddy.

### Step 3: Derive Harmony Colors

Rotate the hue angle to find the accent and secondary colors:

```
Primary hue: 204°

Complementary accent:  204° + 180° = 24°  → warm orange (#FF7A00)
Analogous secondary:   204° + 30°  = 234° → blue-purple (#3B5BDB)
Split-complementary:   204° + 150° = 354° → red (#E03131)
                       204° + 210° = 54°  → yellow-green (#94D82D)
```

For most brands, the complementary color becomes the accent (10% usage). The analogous color becomes the secondary (30% usage).

### Step 4: Generate Neutrals from the Primary Hue

Neutrals derived from the primary hue feel intentional rather than generic. Reduce saturation to 8–15% while keeping the primary hue:

| Token | Lightness | Saturation | Hex (example) | Use |
|-------|-----------|-----------|---------------|-----|
| Neutral 50 | 98% | 10% | `#F8FAFC` | Page background |
| Neutral 100 | 96% | 10% | `#F1F5F9` | Card background |
| Neutral 200 | 90% | 8% | `#E2E8F0` | Borders |
| Neutral 400 | 63% | 8% | `#94A3B8` | Placeholder text |
| Neutral 600 | 40% | 10% | `#475569` | Body text |
| Neutral 800 | 18% | 12% | `#1E293B` | Headings |
| Neutral 900 | 10% | 12% | `#0F172A` | Near-black |

### Step 5: Add Semantic Colors

Semantic colors are independent of the brand palette but must not clash with it. Use established conventions:

| Role | Hex | Notes |
|------|-----|-------|
| Success | `#10B981` | Green; avoid if primary is green — shift to teal |
| Warning | `#F59E0B` | Amber; avoid if primary is orange |
| Error | `#EF4444` | Red; avoid if primary is red — shift to crimson |
| Info | `#3B82F6` | Blue; avoid if primary is blue — use indigo |

## 7. Color Roles in Brand Systems

A complete brand color system assigns every color a defined role. Undefined colors lead to inconsistent application across teams and touchpoints.

| Role | Definition | Typical Hex Range | Usage |
|------|-----------|------------------|-------|
| **Primary** | The brand's most recognizable color; used most frequently | Brand-specific | Logos, primary buttons, key UI elements |
| **Primary Dark** | Shade 700–800 of primary scale | Darker than primary | Hover/active states, dark backgrounds |
| **Primary Light** | Shade 100–200 of primary scale | Lighter than primary | Backgrounds, tinted surfaces |
| **Secondary** | Supports primary; often analogous or triadic | Brand-specific | Navigation, cards, secondary sections |
| **Accent** | High-contrast, attention-grabbing; often complementary | Brand-specific | CTAs, highlights, badges, links |
| **Neutral Dark** | Near-black derived from primary hue | `#0F172A`–`#1E293B` | Body text, headings |
| **Neutral Mid** | Mid-grey derived from primary hue | `#475569`–`#64748B` | Secondary text, captions |
| **Neutral Light** | Near-white derived from primary hue | `#F1F5F9`–`#F8FAFC` | Page backgrounds, card backgrounds |
| **Neutral Border** | Light grey for dividers | `#E2E8F0`–`#CBD5E1` | Borders, dividers, table lines |
| **Success** | Semantic green | `#10B981` | Confirmations, positive states |
| **Warning** | Semantic amber | `#F59E0B` | Caution states, pending |
| **Error** | Semantic red | `#EF4444` | Errors, destructive actions |
| **Info** | Semantic blue | `#3B82F6` | Informational messages |

Document every role in the brand system with its hex, RGB, and CMYK values. Every color that appears in production should map to a named role — no ad-hoc hex values.

## 8. Neutral Scale Generation

Neutrals carry the majority of the visual weight in any brand system. They appear on backgrounds, body text, borders, and disabled states — far more surface area than the primary color. A poorly constructed neutral scale is the most common cause of a brand system that feels "off."

### Warm vs. Cool Neutrals

| Type | Hue Bias | Character | Derived From |
|------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Warm neutral** | Yellow/orange (20–40°) | Approachable, human, organic, inviting | Warm primary (orange, red, yellow) |
| **Cool neutral** | Blue/cyan (200–240°) | Professional, modern, clean, precise | Cool primary (blue, teal, purple) |
| **True neutral** | No hue bias (0° or desaturated) | Universal, versatile, no personality | Any primary at near-zero saturation |

### Construction Method

Derive neutrals from the primary hue by reducing chroma (saturation) to 8–15%. This creates a subtle tonal relationship between the brand color and the neutral surfaces it appears on — the effect is cohesion without distraction.

**Example: Blue primary (#0E96E8, hue 204°)**

```
Neutral scale (HSL, hue locked to 204°):

50:  HSL(204, 10%, 98%)  →  #F8FAFC   Page background
100: HSL(204, 10%, 96%)  →  #F1F5F9   Card background
200: HSL(204,  8%, 90%)  →  #E2E8F0   Borders, dividers
300: HSL(204,  8%, 80%)  →  #CBD5E1   Disabled elements
400: HSL(204,  8%, 63%)  →  #94A3B8   Placeholder text
500: HSL(204, 10%, 53%)  →  #64748B   Secondary text
600: HSL(204, 10%, 40%)  →  #475569   Body text
700: HSL(204, 12%, 29%)  →  #334155   Subheadings
800: HSL(204, 12%, 18%)  →  #1E293B   Headings
900: HSL(204, 12%, 10%)  →  #0F172A   Near-black
```

**Example: Warm primary (#E8600E, hue 24°)**

```
50:  HSL(24, 12%, 98%)  →  #FDFAF8   Warm white
100: HSL(24, 12%, 95%)  →  #FAF5F0   Warm card background
200: HSL(24, 10%, 88%)  →  #EDE0D4   Warm border
600: HSL(24, 10%, 38%)  →  #5C4A3A   Warm body text
900: HSL(24, 12%, 10%)  →  #1C1410   Warm near-black
```

### Neutral Scale Pitfalls

- **Pure grey (#808080) neutrals** feel disconnected from the brand palette. Always introduce a small hue bias.
- **Saturation above 15%** at the light end makes backgrounds feel tinted rather than neutral.
- **Insufficient steps** between 400 and 600 creates contrast problems for text on mid-tone backgrounds.

## 9. WCAG Accessibility

### Contrast Ratio Requirements

WCAG 2.2 defines contrast as a ratio between the relative luminance of two colors. The formula:

```
Contrast Ratio = (L1 + 0.05) / (L2 + 0.05)

Where L1 = relative luminance of the lighter color
      L2 = relative luminance of the darker color
Range: 1:1 (identical) → 21:1 (black on white)
```

| Text Type | AA (legal minimum) | AAA (enhanced) |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------|
| Normal text (< 18pt regular, < 14pt bold) | **4.5:1** | 7:1 |
| Large text (≥ 18pt regular, ≥ 14pt bold) | **3:1** | 4.5:1 |
| UI components and graphics | **3:1** | — |
| Decorative text, logos, inactive UI | No requirement | — |

Level AA is the legal baseline in the EU (European Accessibility Act) and US (Section 508). Target AA for all production work; target AAA for body text when feasible.

### Practical Accessibility Rules

1. Never rely on color alone to convey information — always pair color with a secondary indicator (icon, label, pattern, or position).
2. Test every text/background combination, not just the primary brand color on white.
3. Test all interactive states: default, hover, focus, active, disabled. Disabled states often fail contrast requirements — use 3:1 minimum for disabled text.
4. Check non-text elements: button borders, form field outlines, icon fills, and chart lines all require 3:1 against their adjacent background.
5. A color that passes on white may fail on a brand-colored background — test in context.
6. Approximately 8% of males have red-green color blindness (deuteranopia/protanopia). Never use red and green as the sole differentiators for status (success/error).

### Contrast Targets by Color Role

| Role | Background | Minimum Ratio | Recommended Ratio |
|------|-----------|--------------|------------------|
| Body text | White (#FFFFFF) | 4.5:1 | 7:1 |
| Body text | Light neutral (50–100) | 4.5:1 | 7:1 |
| Heading text | White | 4.5:1 | 7:1 |
| Button label | Primary color | 4.5:1 | 7:1 |
| Placeholder text | White | 3:1 | 4.5:1 |
| Icon (informational) | White | 3:1 | 4.5:1 |
| Border (form field) | White | 3:1 | 3:1 |

### Common Failures and Fixes

| Failure | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Light primary on white fails | Primary shade 400–500 too light | Use shade 600–700 for text on white |
| White text on primary fails | Primary too light | Use primary shade 600+ for backgrounds with white text |
| Accent on primary background fails | Both colors mid-range | Darken accent or lighten background |
| Grey placeholder text fails | Neutral 400 on white = ~3.5:1 | Use neutral 500 minimum for placeholder |

### Verification Tools

- **WebAIM Contrast Checker** — webaim.org/resources/contrastchecker (paste hex values, instant result)
- **Colour Contrast Analyser** — desktop app by TPGi; eyedropper picks colors from any screen
- **Figma Contrast Plugin** — checks contrast in-canvas without leaving the design tool
- **Stark** — Figma/Sketch plugin; checks contrast, simulates color blindness, generates reports

## 10. Programmatic Color Generation

For brand systems that need to generate palettes algorithmically — design system tooling, brand kit generators, or token pipelines — two JavaScript libraries cover the full range of requirements.

### culori (OKLCH-based, recommended)

culori operates in OKLCH (Oklab Lightness-Chroma-Hue), a perceptually uniform color space. Unlike HSL, equal steps in OKLCH produce equal perceived differences in lightness and chroma — no "blue hump" where blues appear darker than reds at the same HSL lightness value. Used by Tailwind CSS v4 and Radix UI.

```
npm install culori
```

Primary use cases:
- Generating perceptually uniform shade scales from a brand hex
- Computing harmony colors by rotating the hue angle
- Checking WCAG contrast ratios via `wcagContrast()`
- Deriving neutrals by reducing chroma to near-zero while preserving hue

### chroma-js (LAB-based, scales and gradients)

chroma-js operates in LAB and supports smooth color scale interpolation. Best suited for generating gradient-based scales, data visualization palettes, and situations where you need to interpolate between two specific brand colors.

```
npm install chroma-js
```

Primary use cases:
- Generating smooth scales between two brand colors
- Computing contrast ratios (`chroma.contrast()` returns the WCAG ratio directly)
- Brightening and darkening colors by perceptual steps
- Desaturating to muted/tonal variants

### When to Use Each

| Task | Library |
|------|---------|
| Generate 9-shade scale from one hex | culori |
| Compute harmony colors (complementary, triadic) | culori |
| Derive neutrals from primary hue | culori |
| Interpolate between two brand colors | chroma-js |
| Generate data visualization palette | chroma-js |
| Check WCAG contrast ratio in code | Either (both support it) |
| Design token pipeline | culori (OKLCH tokens are CSS-native in 2026) |

Full code recipes for palette generation belong in `brand-output.md`. The key principle here: algorithmic generation using OKLCH produces palettes that are perceptually consistent across hues — a manually constructed HSL palette will have uneven perceived lightness across the scale, particularly in the blue and purple ranges.

## Quick Reference: Palette Construction Checklist

Before finalizing any brand palette, verify each item:

- [ ] Primary color chosen and converted to HSL/OKLCH
- [ ] 9-shade scale generated (50–900), saturation reduced at extremes
- [ ] Accent color derived from complementary or split-complementary hue
- [ ] Secondary color derived from analogous hue (if needed)
- [ ] Neutral scale generated from primary hue at 8–15% saturation
- [ ] Semantic colors (success, warning, error, info) defined and non-clashing
- [ ] 60-30-10 distribution mapped to specific UI surfaces
- [ ] All text/background combinations checked against WCAG AA (4.5:1)
- [ ] White text on primary color checked (often requires shade 600+)
- [ ] Primary color on white checked (often requires shade 600+ for body text)
- [ ] Cultural associations verified for target market(s)
- [ ] Color blindness simulation run (red/green differentiation removed)
- [ ] Every color assigned a named role in the brand system
