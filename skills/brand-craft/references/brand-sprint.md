# Brand Sprint Process

Sources: Wheeler (Designing Brand Identity), Johnson (Branding: In Five and a Half Steps), Mark & Pearson (The Hero and the Outlaw), Aaker (Brand Personality Dimensions)

---

A brand sprint compresses weeks of brand strategy work into a focused session. The output is a creative brief — a single document that defines who the brand is, who it serves, and what it stands for — ready to drive visual identity decisions. This file covers the discovery and strategy phase only. Color selection, typography, logo design, and output generation are handled in separate reference files.

---

## Phase Overview: The 5-Phase Brand Sprint

| Phase | Name | Goal | Output |
|-------|------|------|--------|
| 1 | Discover | Understand the brand's context and raw material | Completed discovery questionnaire |
| 2 | Define | Clarify values, audience, and competitive position | Positioning statement |
| 3 | Direct | Assign personality dimensions and archetype | Personality profile + archetype selection |
| 4 | Design | Translate strategy into visual direction | Visual direction brief |
| 5 | Deliver | Consolidate into a single creative brief | Brand brief document |

Work through the phases in order. Each phase feeds the next. Skipping discovery to jump to archetype selection produces generic results.

---

## Phase 1: Discover — The 9 Discovery Questions

Ask the user each question in sequence. Record answers verbatim before interpreting them. Resist the urge to suggest answers — the user's own language is the raw material for naming, voice, and positioning.

### The Discovery Questionnaire

**Q1 — The Business**
"Describe what this brand does in one sentence, as if explaining to someone who has never heard of it."

*Why it matters:* Forces clarity on the core offering. Vague answers signal unclear positioning — a problem to solve in Phase 2, not paper over.

**Q2 — The Audience**
"Who is the primary customer? Describe a specific person, not a demographic segment."

*Why it matters:* Wheeler emphasizes that brand identity must be designed for a specific human, not an abstraction. Push for a named persona: age, occupation, what they care about, what frustrates them.

**Q3 — The Problem**
"What problem does this brand solve for that person? What would their life look like without it?"

*Why it matters:* Reveals the emotional territory. A brand that solves a functional problem (saves time) differs fundamentally from one that solves an identity problem (makes me feel capable).

**Q4 — The Competitors**
"Name 3-5 brands this person might consider instead. What do those brands stand for?"

*Why it matters:* Johnson's "missing gap" framework — the right brand position is the one that is both true and unoccupied. You cannot find the gap without mapping the landscape.

**Q5 — The Differentiator**
"What does this brand do or believe that none of those competitors do or believe?"

*Why it matters:* This is the seed of the positioning statement. If the user cannot answer this, the brand has a strategy problem, not a design problem.

**Q6 — The Values**
"List 3-5 words that describe what this brand stands for. Then list 3-5 words that describe what it absolutely does not stand for."

*Why it matters:* The "does not stand for" list is often more revealing. It defines the brand's edges and prevents the personality from becoming generic.

**Q7 — The Personality**
"If this brand were a person at a dinner party, how would they behave? What would they talk about? What would they never say?"

*Why it matters:* Personality questions unlock voice and tone. The dinner party framing makes abstract values concrete and testable.

**Q8 — The Aspiration**
"Where does this brand want to be in 5 years? What does success look like?"

*Why it matters:* Reveals whether the brand needs to project confidence it hasn't yet earned (startup positioning) or consolidate authority it already has (established brand repositioning).

**Q9 — The Reference**
"Name 2-3 brands — in any industry — that you admire aesthetically or strategically. What specifically do you admire about each?"

*Why it matters:* Reference brands reveal unstated preferences faster than any direct question. Note: the goal is to understand the emotional register, not to copy the visual style.

### Discovery Red Flags

| Answer Pattern | What It Signals | How to Respond |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| "We serve everyone" | No audience clarity | Push for the primary customer; secondary audiences come later |
| "We're better quality" | No real differentiator | Ask: better than whom, and provably how? |
| "We're like [big brand] but..." | Derivative positioning | Explore what the big brand does NOT do |
| Contradictory values | Internal misalignment | Surface the contradiction; resolve before proceeding |
| No competitors named | Denial or genuine novelty | If genuine novelty, focus on category creation |

## Phase 2: Define — The Positioning Statement

The positioning statement is the strategic spine of the brand. Every visual and verbal decision should be traceable back to it.

### The Formula

```
For [specific audience],
[brand name] is the [category]
that [key benefit]
because [reason to believe].
```

### Filling the Formula

**[Specific audience]:** Use the persona from Q2. Be specific enough that someone could picture the person.

**[Category]:** The competitive frame. This tells the audience how to think about the brand. Choosing the category is a strategic decision — a brand can reframe itself by choosing a different category (e.g., "the project management tool" vs. "the team communication platform").

**[Key benefit]:** One benefit only. The benefit that matters most to the audience and that the brand can own. Functional benefits are easier to copy; emotional and identity benefits are more defensible.

**[Reason to believe]:** The proof point. What makes the benefit credible? This can be a process, a technology, a heritage, a community, or a philosophy.

### Positioning Statement Examples

| Brand Type | Example Statement |
|------------|-------------------|
| B2B SaaS | For operations managers at mid-size logistics companies, FlowDesk is the workflow automation platform that eliminates approval bottlenecks because it maps to how teams actually work, not how software assumes they do. |
| Consumer food | For health-conscious parents who distrust ingredient labels, Grove is the snack brand that makes clean eating effortless because every ingredient is named, sourced, and explained on the pack. |
| Creative agency | For founders who need a brand before their Series A, Meridian is the brand studio that delivers investor-ready identity in 10 days because we've built the same brief-to-delivery system 200 times. |
| Nonprofit | For urban youth aged 14-18 who lack access to professional mentors, Ladder is the career development program that opens doors to industries that feel out of reach because our mentors are practitioners, not volunteers. |

### Common Positioning Mistakes

**Benefit stacking:** "that saves time, reduces costs, and improves team morale" — pick one. The others become supporting proof points, not the headline benefit.

**Category too broad:** "the software that helps businesses" — narrow the category until it creates a clear competitive frame.

**Reason to believe missing:** Without proof, the benefit is a claim. Claims are forgettable; proof is memorable.

**Audience too wide:** "for professionals" — this is not an audience. Narrow until the statement would feel irrelevant to someone outside the target.

## Phase 3: Direct — Brand Personality Profiling

### Aaker's 5 Dimensions as Sliders

Jennifer Aaker's five-dimension model (Stanford, 1997) provides a structured vocabulary for brand personality. Map the brand on each dimension using a 1-5 scale, where 1 is the left pole and 5 is the right pole.

| Dimension | 1 (Low) | 3 (Neutral) | 5 (High) | Core Traits |
|-----------|---------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| **Sincerity** | Formal, distant | Balanced | Warm, genuine | Down-to-earth, honest, wholesome, cheerful |
| **Excitement** | Steady, conservative | Balanced | Bold, energetic | Daring, spirited, imaginative, current |
| **Competence** | Casual, informal | Balanced | Expert, authoritative | Reliable, intelligent, successful, confident |
| **Sophistication** | Accessible, democratic | Balanced | Refined, exclusive | Upper-class, charming, glamorous, smooth |
| **Ruggedness** | Polished, urban | Balanced | Raw, outdoorsy | Tough, strong, outdoorsy, masculine |

### How to Score the Dimensions

Use the discovery answers to score each dimension. Do not ask the user to score directly — infer from their language and reference brands.

- Q6 (values) and Q7 (personality) are the primary inputs
- Q9 (reference brands) calibrates the scores against known examples
- The "does not stand for" list from Q6 often defines the low end of each dimension

### Personality Profile Examples

| Brand Type | Sincerity | Excitement | Competence | Sophistication | Ruggedness |
|------------|-----------|------------|------------|----------------|------------|
| Enterprise SaaS | 3 | 2 | 5 | 3 | 1 |
| Outdoor apparel | 4 | 3 | 3 | 1 | 5 |
| Luxury skincare | 2 | 2 | 4 | 5 | 1 |
| Youth fitness app | 3 | 5 | 3 | 2 | 3 |
| Community nonprofit | 5 | 2 | 3 | 1 | 2 |
| Creative agency | 3 | 4 | 4 | 3 | 1 |

### Reading the Profile

The two highest-scoring dimensions define the brand's primary personality territory. The lowest-scoring dimension defines what the brand is explicitly not — this is as important as what it is.

A brand scoring 5 on Competence and 1 on Ruggedness (IBM, McKinsey) communicates through precision, structure, and authority. A brand scoring 5 on Ruggedness and 1 on Sophistication (Carhartt, Timberland) communicates through rawness, durability, and anti-polish.

## Phase 4: Archetype Quick-Select

The personality profile maps directly to one or two archetypes from Mark & Pearson's framework. Use the table below to identify the primary archetype, then check for a secondary archetype that adds depth without contradiction.

### Archetype Decision Table

| Dominant Dimensions | Primary Archetype | Secondary Options | Avoid |
|--------------------|-------------------|-------------------|-------|
| High Sincerity + Low Excitement | Innocent or Caregiver | Everyman | Outlaw, Ruler |
| High Excitement + High Ruggedness | Explorer or Outlaw | Hero | Innocent, Lover |
| High Competence + Low Ruggedness | Sage or Ruler | Creator | Jester, Innocent |
| High Sophistication + Low Sincerity | Lover or Ruler | Magician | Jester, Everyman |
| High Excitement + Low Sophistication | Jester or Outlaw | Explorer | Ruler, Sage |
| High Sincerity + High Competence | Caregiver or Sage | Everyman | Outlaw, Jester |
| High Excitement + High Competence | Hero or Magician | Creator | Innocent, Caregiver |
| High Ruggedness + High Sincerity | Explorer or Hero | Everyman | Lover, Ruler |
| Balanced across all dimensions | Everyman | Caregiver | Any extreme archetype |
| High Sophistication + High Competence | Ruler or Magician | Sage | Jester, Innocent |

### Archetype Quick Reference

| Archetype | Core Promise | Competitive Category | Example Brands |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Innocent | Simple goodness, purity | Consumer goods, personal care | Dove, Aveeno, Innocent Drinks |
| Explorer | Freedom, discovery | Outdoor, travel, vehicles | Patagonia, REI, Jeep |
| Sage | Knowledge, truth | Education, consulting, media | Google, BBC, TED, McKinsey |
| Hero | Achievement, mastery | Sports, performance, military | Nike, FedEx, Gatorade |
| Outlaw | Disruption, liberation | Counter-culture, challenger brands | Harley-Davidson, Liquid Death, Virgin |
| Magician | Transformation, wonder | Technology, entertainment, beauty | Apple, Disney, Tesla |
| Lover | Intimacy, pleasure | Beauty, luxury, food indulgence | Chanel, Häagen-Dazs, L'Oréal |
| Jester | Joy, play | Consumer goods, food, entertainment | M&Ms, Old Spice, Ben & Jerry's |
| Everyman | Belonging, accessibility | Mass retail, value brands | IKEA, Target, Levi's |
| Caregiver | Protection, nurturing | Healthcare, nonprofits, insurance | Johnson & Johnson, UNICEF, Volvo |
| Creator | Originality, craft | Creative tools, arts, design | Adobe, LEGO, Etsy |
| Ruler | Authority, order | Luxury, finance, premium B2B | Rolex, American Express, Mercedes |

### Primary + Shadow Archetype

Most strong brands have a primary archetype and a secondary "shadow" archetype that adds nuance. The shadow should complement, not contradict, the primary.

| Primary | Compatible Shadow | Example |
|---------|------------------|---------|
| Explorer | Outlaw | Patagonia (Explorer + anti-consumerism Outlaw) |
| Sage | Creator | Google (Sage + Creator) |
| Hero | Ruler | Nike (Hero + Ruler in premium lines) |
| Caregiver | Innocent | Johnson & Johnson (Caregiver + Innocent) |
| Magician | Creator | Apple (Magician + Creator) |
| Jester | Outlaw | Old Spice (Jester + Outlaw irreverence) |

Incompatible pairings to avoid: Innocent + Outlaw, Ruler + Jester, Sage + Jester, Lover + Ruggedness-dominant archetypes.

## Phase 5: Creative Brief Output

The sprint produces a single-page creative brief. This document is the handoff from strategy to design. Every visual decision — color, typography, logo style — should be traceable to a line in this brief.

### Creative Brief Structure

**Brand Name:** [Name]

**Positioning Statement:** [From Phase 2]

**Primary Audience:** [Specific persona from Q2]

**Core Benefit:** [Single benefit from positioning statement]

**Brand Personality Profile:**
- Sincerity: [1-5]
- Excitement: [1-5]
- Competence: [1-5]
- Sophistication: [1-5]
- Ruggedness: [1-5]

**Primary Archetype:** [Name] — [one-sentence description of how it applies]

**Shadow Archetype (if applicable):** [Name] — [one-sentence description]

**Brand Voice:** [3-5 adjectives derived from personality profile and Q7]

**Brand Voice Does Not:** [3-5 things the brand never says or does, from Q6]

**Visual Direction:** [3-4 sentences translating archetype and personality into visual territory — mood, not specific colors or fonts]

**Reference Brands:** [From Q9, with specific notes on what to borrow and what to avoid]

**Competitive Differentiation:** [One sentence on the gap this brand occupies, from Q4-Q5]

### Visual Direction Translation

The visual direction section bridges strategy and design. Write it in terms of emotional register and visual qualities, not specific design decisions. Specific decisions (color, type, logo style) are made in the design phase using the color-strategy, typography-system, and logo-creation reference files.

| Archetype | Visual Direction Language |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| Innocent | Clean, open, light. Softness over sharpness. Space over density. Warmth without sentimentality. |
| Explorer | Expansive, textured, grounded. Movement implied. Nothing too polished or precious. |
| Sage | Structured, precise, authoritative. Hierarchy matters. Restraint signals intelligence. |
| Hero | Bold, dynamic, high-contrast. Energy through tension. Nothing passive or decorative. |
| Outlaw | Raw, unconventional, high-contrast. Rules broken deliberately. Imperfection as authenticity. |
| Magician | Depth, mystery, transformation. Rich surfaces. The unexpected made beautiful. |
| Lover | Sensual, refined, intimate. Curves over angles. Warmth over coolness. Detail rewarded. |
| Jester | Energetic, playful, surprising. Color as joy. Asymmetry and bounce. Nothing stiff. |
| Everyman | Honest, approachable, unpretentious. Familiar without being boring. Warmth over cool. |
| Caregiver | Soft, embracing, reassuring. Nothing sharp or cold. Warmth is structural, not decorative. |
| Creator | Expressive, original, craft-forward. The handmade valued. Distinctive over conventional. |
| Ruler | Commanding, precise, premium. Symmetry and structure. Restraint signals authority. |

## Brand Naming Guidance

Naming is relevant when the brand does not yet have a name, or when the sprint reveals that the existing name conflicts with the desired positioning. Apply these principles when naming is in scope.

### Naming Categories

| Category | Description | Strengths | Weaknesses | Examples |
|----------|-------------|-----------|------------|---------|
| **Descriptive** | States what the brand does | Immediately clear | Hard to trademark; generic | General Electric, American Airlines |
| **Suggestive** | Implies a benefit or quality | Memorable, trademarkable | Requires some explanation | Slack, Stripe, Zoom |
| **Abstract** | Invented or meaningless word | Highly trademarkable, ownable | Requires investment to build meaning | Kodak, Xerox, Häagen-Dazs |
| **Acronym** | Initials of longer name | Compact | Requires established recognition | IBM, BMW, HSBC |
| **Founder** | Named after founder(s) | Heritage, accountability | Limits brand beyond founder | Ford, Disney, Chanel |
| **Metaphor** | Borrows meaning from another domain | Evocative, memorable | Can be misread | Amazon, Apple, Patagonia |

### Naming Principles

**Principle 1 — Pronounceability.** If a name cannot be said aloud without hesitation, it will not spread by word of mouth. Test by asking three people to read it cold.

**Principle 2 — Distinctiveness.** The name must be ownable in its category. Search the USPTO trademark database and Google before committing. A name that is already in use in the same category is a legal and strategic liability.

**Principle 3 — Scalability.** Avoid names that lock the brand into a specific product, geography, or technology. "Boston Consulting" works; "Boston Web Consulting" does not scale.

**Principle 4 — Archetype alignment.** The name's sound and associations should reinforce the archetype. Hard consonants (K, X, T) project strength and confidence (Hero, Ruler). Soft consonants and open vowels (L, M, O, A) project warmth and approachability (Caregiver, Innocent).

**Principle 5 — Domain and handle availability.** Check .com availability and social handle availability before finalizing. A name with no available .com requires a workaround that adds friction to every brand touchpoint.

### Name Evaluation Checklist

| Criterion | Pass Condition |
|-----------|---------------|
| Pronounceable on first read | Yes, by 3 out of 3 test readers |
| Memorable after 24 hours | Recalled without prompting |
| Trademark clear in category | No conflicting marks in USPTO / relevant jurisdiction |
| .com available or workable alternative | Yes |
| Archetype-aligned sound | Consonants and vowels match emotional register |
| No negative connotations in target markets | Verified across primary languages and cultures |
| Scalable beyond current product | Does not describe a specific product or geography |

## Sprint Quality Gates

Before moving from strategy to design, verify these conditions are met. A brief that fails any gate will produce a visual identity that cannot be defended or extended.

| Gate | Condition | If Failing |
|------|-----------|------------|
| Positioning clarity | Statement passes the "so what?" test | Return to Q3-Q5; sharpen the differentiator |
| Audience specificity | A real person can be pictured | Return to Q2; push for a named persona |
| Personality coherence | Dimension scores do not contradict each other | Return to Q6-Q7; resolve the contradiction |
| Archetype fit | Archetype matches personality profile and positioning | Re-run the archetype table with corrected scores |
| Competitive differentiation | Position is unoccupied by named competitors | Return to Q4-Q5; find the missing gap |
| Visual direction | Brief gives enough direction to make color and type decisions | Expand the visual direction section |

A brief that passes all six gates is ready to hand off to the design phase. Proceed to color-strategy.md for color selection, typography-system.md for type decisions, and logo-creation.md for mark development.
